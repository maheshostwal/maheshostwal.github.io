Potluck

Overview

The goal of the project was to research and design a solution to help new international students in the US deal with food neophobia and try out new foods.



My Role

Led the team of 4 through the design phase of the project and contributed equally to each of the other phases

Problem Space

  • New international students in the US are unable to decide on foods looking at the menu
  • They are very cautious in trying out new foods

Target User Group

New international students at US universities



User Research

The Process

1. Naturalistic Observation

  • Conducted several 30 minutes field study sessions at the various on-campus dining places
  • Observed for student behavior, body language, facial expressions and oral communication

We conducted naturalistic observation sessions of 30 minutes each at the various on-campus eating places



2. Task Analysis

  • The task of ordering food was analyzed based on the naturalistic observations
  • The task steps vary according to the place of dining such as cafeteria, dining hall etc.
The common characteristics among the various dining places:
  • Limited time
  • Finite set of options
  • Little information about the foods
  • Noisy and crowded environment

The Task Analysis diagram



3. Contextual Inquiry

We conducted several contextual inquiries with the target user group to understand “why” they do “what” they do Accompanied participants to different on-campus dining places

We conducted numerous contextual inquiries with international students


4. Affinity Mapping

This was done based on the notes gathered from the naturalistic observations and contextual inquiries Some of the important findings:
Some of the important findings are as follows:

  • 1) Menus are uninformative
  • 2) Peers advice and opinions are highly valued
  • 3) Students are very open to try out new foods
  • 4) Familiar foods and tastes are preferred
  • 5) Time is an important factor

Holodeck

What is the problem?
The director of the MS-HCI program feels that students need to use too many different applications to manage their HCI concerns Students need to be able to communicate and access information related to their academic and professional careers
Mission: Investigate MS-HCI student needs and the systems used to address those needs to develop a more comprehensive resource
Who are HCI Students?
Masters of Human-Computer Interaction program at Georgia Tech 4 Tracks of study: Interactive Computing, Industrial Design, Digital Media, and Psychology 111 total students (56 first-year, 55 second-year) More than half are international students
Research Objectives
Understand the behaviors, attitudes, and goals of HCI students Identify the information and communication needs of HCI students Evaluate the current systems in place to address these needs Determine to what extent the current systems meet these needs

Conclusion
The creation of a ‘one-stop shop’, as outlined by the Director of the Human-Computer Interaction Master’s program, is a feasible undertaking. Evidence suggests that there is a real need for a centralized resource. Communication tools in use are effective in meeting HCI student needs. Slack is used for this purpose by the entire program and the experience of students using this application is overwhelmingly positive. This application can be used as a foundation for the communication tool provided by the ‘one-stop shop’. Resource access is made difficult by the number of applications/sites involved and the limitations of class and institutional requirements. HCI students report feeling confused about the various sources needed to find important resources. A ‘one-stop shop’ should allow users to import important information found on other sites into a single, easily organized location. Other tools- like class information and portfolio development- are not available to students. In these cases, new resources must be developed. It is possible to build these resources by utilizing the strong peer community and preference of many students to ask peers for help.
